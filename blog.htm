Why are subtitles so different from the original?

October 8, 2019 | Reading time: 8 minutes
Written by Victoria Cabral

We have all been there, I know. Sometimes we are watching a super cool foreign movie or TV show and then... we feel deceived. If the character is saying THESE many words, why are the subtitles so short? I mean, where is all that information? And why is the dubbed version so different from the subtitles? So many questions, am I right?

In this article, I want to answer all your questions by going over the fundamentals of subtitling.

Let's start off with basic concepts. Subtitles are the text displayed at the bottom of audiovisual content, such as movies and TV shows. The audience does not speak the source language, so the text you see is a translation of what people are saying in the video. However, there are other types of subtitles: Closed Captions (CC) and Subtitles for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing (SDH). Closed Captions, also known as CC or Captions, are transcriptions in the same language as the source material. In TV channels, Closed Captions are usually automatically generated by professional tools which use AI to identify who is speaking. In the United States, the law determines that 100% of audiovisual content must have Closed Captions. Subtitles for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing (aka SDH) and Closed Captions are made by humans and describe all sound details in audiovisual content. If there is a knock on a door, viewers may see something like [Knock on door] or [Jane knocks on door] on their screen, for example. The sounds are interpreted, such as [Happy music plays in the background]. Although each country has its own rules, SDH usually condenses information to give the audience time to process what is being said, unlike Closed Captions.

Okay, but still... Why are the subtitles so substantially different from what the characters are saying?

To answer that, I need to talk to you about guidelines. Viewers must be able to read the subtitles comfortably. Therefore, companies, TV channels, and translation agencies adhere to strict guidelines to ensure quality and readability. Subtitles have a minimum and maximum exposure time on your screen. Similarly, each subtitle has a maximum number of characters per second, per minute, and per line. Allow me to explain: any letter, number, symbol, or space is a character, and there is only so much information we can process in a certain amount of time. As a result, subtitles need to summarize what people are saying. Subtitlers aim to make sentences shorter while also trying to convey the original meaning. To do so, they cut off interjections, hesitations, and some greetings, for example. This is only possible because we see (and often also hear) what is happening.

How to Spot Translation Scams From a Mile Away

May 21, 2021 | Reading time: 7 minutes
Written by Ji Sung-Ho

The complete guide to never fall for a translation scam.


When the Internet became a thing, translators were able to work from anywhere in the world. Using online dictionaries and termbases, finding clients around the globe, and connecting with colleagues is fantastic, right? But not everything is a bed of roses. Criminals can get creative to steal your information. There are fake job ads, fake Project Managers, fake agencies, fake princes, and who knows what else out there. We have good news, though: avoiding and spotting those scams is easier than you think.

1. Always Google the person you are speaking to

No matter if they contacted you on LinkedIn, Proz, or if they sent you an e-mail. Just Google their name, the company they claim to work for, and, if you have it, their e-mail address. Companies with free e-mail accounts, no website, and no social media presence are for sure a scam. You have to see who they are and how long they have been in the Translation Industry, at least.

2. Look into The ProZ.com Blue Board

As described in their website, the Proz Blue Board "is a database of translation agencies, companies and outsourcers with feedback from language service providers". Translators have been rating clients on the Blue Board since 2001. If the company offering you a job doesn’t even exist on Proz, it’s not a good sign.

3. Acess Payment Practices

Several translators consider Payment Practices to be an invaluable resource. There is information about agencies dating back to 1999 on their website. For less than 20 dollars a year, you can avoid losing time, money, and effort in both scams and bad clients. It’s a must-see before agreeing to any translation job.

4. Translator Scammers Directory

This website will give you everything you need to know about translator scammers. They have countless useful tabs, such as “How to Read E-Mails’ Headers”, "The Scams Explained", "Scammers' Bank Accounts", "Fake CVs Gallery" and "How To Protect Yourself". If you come across a scammer that is not listed in there, please, speak up. And remember: you can never pay to work for a client, even if "it's just $20"!

5. Ask your colleagues

The Translation community is so big on social media like LinkedIn, Facebook, and Proz. There are a lot of people willing to help. Ask around if somebody you know already worked for this agency, or if they ever heard of the person who contacted you. Who knows, it could also be a networking opportunity.

What can I do?

First, never post any documents online. This means certificates, diplomas, Ids, passports, and even your CV. If you absolutely have to send out document scans, put a watermark on top of your name and photo. Identify the recipient (as in "Sent to Blue Diamond Agency on May 2020") and use an artistic font to make it harder for anybody to remove the writing. Also, ask for a video chat (on Skype, Zoom, or Google Meet, for example).

Translation Agencies Insights on Freelance Translators’ CVs

November 7, 2015 | Reading time: 6 minutes
Written by Gabriela Montoya

At some point, every freelance translator will ask themselves:

“How can I build a good CV?”
“Is my CV any good?”
"What should I include in my freelance translator resumé?"
“What do Translation Agencies look for in freelance translators' CVs?”

This is a sign that you care about your profession and that you want to be your best version. Today, we’ll bring you a few tips and resources on how to write a good translator CV from the Translation Agency perspective.

Before anything, a good resource is this free e-book written by Marta Stelmaszak. It's called "You Need A CV That Works". She goes over every section and mistake that she has seen in freelance translators' CVs. She also explains how to write cover letters and personal statements. Don't miss it! Also, be sure to check out Tess Witty interviewing Marta for the Marketing Tips for Translators Podcast!

Let's start with the basics:

1. Do not post your CV online

Scammers can't have easy access to your CV. If you really want to post it on Proz and LinkedIn, post a basic version only. Including your name, country, and specializations is more than enough. Write "Full CV available upon request" in it.

2. Make a spreadsheet with every place you sent your CV

It's easier for you to control who you talked to and if they sent you any tests, for instance, if you have a simple spreadsheet. If your CV ever gets stolen, you will know exactly where you sent it.

3. Never send your CV in .Docx

It's just not professional. Apart from that, if your CV comes across translators scammers, they will steal it in no time. The standard CV format in the Translation Industry is PDF. In addition, after saving your CV in a PDF format, convert the text inside it to an image format, such as JPG or PNG. You can do this online for free. If your CV gets stolen, the criminals will have a hard time redesigning it.

4. Make the best out of the Internet

Nowadays, nobody prints out CVs anymore. Since you're sending an online file, make the most of it. Add links to your contact information and your portfolio, for instance. If you're an interpreter, link a sample of your voice. It makes all the difference.

5. Have your own domain

Even if you don't plan to have a website, have your own domain. You will look more professional and will also avoid having your identity stolen if you let everyone know that all of your professional e-mails will come from that domain.

6. Always personalize your e-mails and cover letters!

We cannot stress this enough. Please, do not send your CV to a hundred agencies at once. Take your time to research each agency. Find out who is receiving your e-mail. Tell them why do you want to freelance with the company. This is how you show that you're a detail-oriented person, which is super important to us when looking for freelancers.

Essential Tools for Translators

August 9, 2018 | Reading time: 4 minutes
Written by Alan Kim

Despite what people might think, being a translator is not easy. There are so many stages in the translation process. Apart from that, if you’re a freelancer, you have to deal with marketing, invoices, and everything else. The right tools can make your life easier by assuring quality, managing terminology, increasing typing speed, organizing teamwork, controlling your finances, and more. We gathered a few essential tools to show you.

1. Xbench - Quality Assurance

Most of the CAT Tools have their own QA Tool, I know. However, doing a double-check is always best, right? Xbench saves you time by spotting inconsistencies, spelling mistakes, and typos. It also generates a report that you can use to see how you can improve. Take a look at their website for more information.

2. The Free Dictionary - Optimized Search

The Free Dictionary is gold. I mean it. It gives you standard dictionary definitions, Wikipedia entries, a Medical Dictionary, a Legal Dictionary, a Financial Dictionary, Idioms, and meanings in different languages. See for yourself here.

3. Google Keep, Evernote, and Trello - Organization

Those are different apps, but their purpose is the same: to help you get organized. Although each one of them has its own functions, the basic ones are creating notes, lists, and boards, syncing with different devices, setting up reminders, and more. Click their names for more info.

4. Canva, Design Wizard, and Crello - Visual Identity and Content Creation

Those websites/apps help you with your brand. You can find templates for business cards, flyers, headers for social media like Facebook and LinkedIn, presentations, holiday cards, and whatever you need. Canva, Design Wizard, and Crello are free and very intuitive. Give them a try.

5. Unsplash, Freepik, and Brasil com S - Free Image Banks

You can use images and illustrations from those websites for free in your blog posts, flyers, and presentations. All you have to do is credit the authors.

What is Localization?

Dec 7, 2020 | Reading time: 10 minutes
Written by Vivian Ji

Yes, there is s difference between Translation and Localization. While Translating is expressing the ideas of a verbal or non-verbal text in a different language, Localization goes the extra mile. In the Translation Industry, Localization refers to the process of technically and linguistically adapting content aiming at a certain persona. The objective of Localization is to make the user feel like that content was developed in their native language.

Localization is crucial for apps, software, websites, and games for two reasons.

First, localizing content enables a company to sell to other countries effectively. After all, Localizing is not only considering the target culture but also the target market. Some cultures want funny and short content, while others only accept serious and longer content. According to the 2020 Global Multilingual CX Report, 68% of consumers would switch to a different brand that offers support in their native language. Not localizing your content could set a brand back on selling in a given country.

Second, Localization involves many people to ensure maximum quality. The Localization process has many stages. Some of those stages are studying the content beforehand, building a termbase, proofreading, and testing the Localized content. Nothing about Localization is automatic.
